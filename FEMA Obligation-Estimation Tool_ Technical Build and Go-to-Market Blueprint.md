# Building and Marketing a FEMA Obligation-Estimation Tool: A Technical and Go-to-Market Blueprint

## TL;DR

- **Build a two-stage (“hurdle”) machine-learning model that estimates FEMA Public Assistance and Individual Assistance obligated dollars at the county-disaster level** from hazard parameters plus exposure and bureaucratic features — this is technically feasible on entirely public data (OpenFEMA + NOAA + USACE/USGS), is academically validated for the upper-Midwest small-event niche the user is targeting, and occupies genuine white space because HAZUS, the National Risk Index, and commercial cat models predict *physical/economic loss* or *annualized risk*, not *FEMA obligations*.
- **The most valuable buyers are disaster-recovery consulting firms (Hagerty, Tidal Basin, IEM, Tetra Tech, ICF, CDR) who scope and price FEMA PA engagements**, followed by govtech/cat-risk firms hiring for the skill set; the tool’s relevance is sharply rising because the May 7, 2026 FEMA Review Council report proposes replacing PA reimbursement with a *parametric block grant (“RAPID”)* tied to wind speed/flood depth — exactly the event-condition-to-dollars mapping this tool performs.
- **Treat this as a portfolio/credibility asset, not a product to sell while employed**: ship a transparent, public, interactive demo on public data, but first clear DHS/FEMA ethics rules — prior written approval (5 CFR 4601.103), the FEMA-contractor employment bar (5 CFR 4601.105), OGE-450 disclosure, the nonpublic-information prohibition (5 CFR 2635.703), and the absolute 18 U.S.C. 205 bar on representing clients before FEMA.

-----

## Key Findings

1. **The prediction target is legitimate and has been done.** A 2024 open-access *Natural Hazards* paper (Ghaedi, Best, Reilly, Niemeier, DOI 10.1007/s11069-024-06620-2) used statistical learning on repetitive upper-Midwest flooding (2003–2018) to predict county-level PA applicants and projects, finding that *non-hazard* features — state, the county’s prior disaster experience, median income, and the lag between incident end and declaration date — are key predictors of PA outlays. This both validates the user’s exact niche (upper-Midwest small events) and warns that bureaucratic/behavioral features matter as much as hazard intensity.
1. **The data linkage is the hard part, and it is solvable with documented join keys.** OpenFEMA’s Public Assistance Funded Projects Details v2, PA Applicants, IHP datasets, and Disaster Declarations Summaries v2 all share the disaster number and FIPS/state-county geography; NOAA Storm Events, USGS streamgages, and hazard swaths join on county + date. The central challenge is that FEMA obligations are recorded at applicant/project level and reconcile over *years*, while hazard data is event/point level — requiring careful temporal and spatial aggregation.
1. **Disaster cost is a skewed, zero-inflated distribution, which dictates the modeling approach.** The right toolkit is a two-stage hurdle/zero-inflated model (classify whether cost occurs, then regress magnitude on log scale) or a Tweedie-loss gradient-boosting model (XGBoost/LightGBM/CatBoost). This is exactly the approach in the peer-reviewed tornado-damage literature and the actuarial insurance-loss literature.
1. **The white space is FEMA-obligation specificity.** HAZUS estimates physical/economic loss from engineering damage functions; the National Risk Index produces *expected annual loss* (exposure × frequency × historic loss ratio); commercial cat models (Verisk, Moody’s RMS, CoreLogic) estimate *insured* loss for underwriting. None of them answer the consultant’s and CFO’s actual question: “Given this event, how many federal dollars will ultimately be obligated?”
1. **Market timing is unusually favorable.** The FEMA Review Council’s final report (May 7, 2026) recommends replacing PA with a parametric “RAPID” block grant paying states within 30 days based on pre-defined event criteria (wind speed, flood depth), and consolidating IA into a single direct payment (“FAIR”). If adopted, designing and auditing those parametric triggers becomes a live commercial need — and a FEMA-obligation model is the natural calibration engine.

## Details

### PART A — TECHNICAL BUILD

#### A1. Methodology for predicting PA/IA obligated dollars

**Frame it as a hurdle/two-stage problem.** Disaster cost data has a large mass at or near zero (most county-events generate little or no obligation) and a long right tail (a few events dominate). The canonical solution, demonstrated directly for weather damage by Diaz & Joseph’s “Predicting property damage from tornadoes with zero-inflated neural networks” (*Weather and Climate Extremes*, arXiv 1807.03456), is a zero-inflated/hurdle structure: one model predicts *whether* damage occurs (they report out-of-sample accuracy 0.821, AUROC 0.872) and a second predicts *how much*, conditional on damage occurring  (they report out-of-sample R² = 0.432 on log-scale, with the explicit caveat that log-scale fitting produces large natural-scale residuals). That R² is a sobering, honest benchmark: even a well-built model explains under half the variance of damage magnitude.

**Recommended estimator stack:**

- **Baseline:** Generalized linear models (log-link gamma for the severity stage, logistic for the occurrence stage) — interpretable, the approach used in the FEMA PA outlays paper and the IHP-equity paper (OLS on log assistance).
- **Primary:** Gradient-boosted trees with a **Tweedie loss** (compound Poisson-gamma), which natively handles the zero mass and right skew of aggregate loss in a single model. The actuarial literature (e.g., “Zero-Inflated Tweedie Boosted Trees with CatBoost,” arXiv 2406.16206; “Tweedie Gradient Boosting for Extremely Unbalanced Zero-inflated Data,” arXiv 1811.10192) shows Tweedie-loss boosting outperforms two-part models for insurance loss and gives unified, interpretable feature effects.  XGBoost, LightGBM, and CatBoost all expose a Tweedie objective.
- **Analog/nearest-neighbor layer:** Because end users (consultants, emergency managers) trust *comparables*, pair the statistical model with a case-based “find the 5 most similar prior disasters” retrieval (k-NN on standardized hazard + exposure features), surfacing the actual historical obligations of analog events. This is both a sanity check and a UX/storytelling feature that differentiates from black-box scores.

**Handling the sparse-event problem (the user’s core constraint).** For Region 5 small events there are relatively few comparable past disasters. Mitigations, in priority order:

- **Pool across the region and across hazard types**, then use hierarchical/multilevel models (partial pooling) so a data-poor county “borrows strength” from the regional distribution — conceptually mirroring the **Bayesian credibility** weighting FEMA itself uses in the National Risk Index’s Historic Loss Ratio methodology (documented in the NRI Technical Documentation).
- **Model at the project/applicant grain** (PA Funded Projects Details has hundreds of thousands of project worksheets nationally), which dramatically increases the effective sample size versus modeling one number per disaster.
- **Transfer learning / national pre-training:** fit the magnitude model on national data, then fine-tune or add region fixed effects for Region 5 — exactly the strategy ORNL used for USA Structures building extraction across data-poor regions. 
- Be explicit that for genuinely rare severe events the model extrapolates and should report wide intervals.

#### A2. The training-data linkage (the crux)

**FEMA side (the dollar targets), all on OpenFEMA:**

- **Public Assistance Funded Projects Details v2** — obligated project worksheets with Federal Share Obligated, damage category code, applicant, county, disaster number. Critical data-quality caveats stated by FEMA: it is raw data from FAC-Trax/Grants Manager subject to “a small percentage of human error,” is *not* from FEMA’s official financial system, may differ from USASpending, and **excludes open/pre-obligation projects** — so recent disasters are systematically under-counted until they mature (obligations reconcile “over the life of the project — sometimes years after the declaration date”). (Note: v1 was retired October 6, 2025; build against v2.)
- **PA Applicants** (join via Applicant ID), **Individuals and Households Program Valid Registrations v2** and **IA datasets** (housing assistance + other needs assistance) for the IA target, **Disaster Declarations Summaries v2** (the spine), and **HMA datasets** for the 406/404 mitigation layer.
- **Join spine:** `disasterNumber` + state + county FIPS. Disaster Declarations Summaries supplies incident type, incident begin/end dates, declared programs, and the `femaDeclarationString` (e.g., DR-4393-NC). Note the numbering convention (1–1999 and 4000+ = major disasters; 2000–2999 = fire management; 3000–3999 = emergency). 

**Event-condition side (the features):**

- **NOAA/NCEI Storm Events Database** — per-event property and crop damage, magnitude (hail size, wind speed), tornado EF/path. Joins on county + date. **Major data-quality warning:** NCEI states damage figures are a “best guess” / “broad estimate,” not adjusted for inflation, sourced variably from NWS, local officials, media, and insurers; researchers (arXiv 1606.06973) found up to 62% missing crop-damage values post-2012 rebuild, and a *Conversation*/*Invading Sea* (2025) analysis argues NWS lacks standardized damage-estimation procedures across local offices. Treat Storm Events damage as a noisy *feature*, never as ground truth for the *target*.
- **USGS streamgages / NWIS** for flood crest height and flood-stage exceedance (already used as a parametric flood trigger by the Mississippi River Cities & Towns Initiative).
- **NOAA MRMS radar, hail/wind swaths**, NWS storm reports, and **fire perimeters** (NIFC) for spatial hazard footprints.

**Known matching challenges to disclose:** (1) temporal mismatch — instantaneous hazard vs. multi-year obligation reconciliation; (2) spatial mismatch — point/path hazard vs. county-level obligations vs. census-block exposure; (3) the declaration filter — only declared events generate FEMA obligations, creating selection bias (the model only “sees” events severe enough to be declared, which is itself partly a political/bureaucratic decision, per the PA outlays paper); (4) deobligations and version churn in PA projects.

#### A3. Feature engineering

Predictive feature families, grounded in the literature:

- **Hazard intensity:** flood depth/extent, peak wind, hail diameter, tornado EF-scale and path length/width, fire perimeter acreage, snowfall/ice accumulation, streamgage crest above flood stage.
- **Exposure:** structure counts and replacement value from **USA Structures** (ORNL/FEMA inventory of 125M+ structures >450 sq ft, with occupancy class)   and the **USACE National Structure Inventory (NSI 2022)** (site-level structure value, occupancy, foundation height — the basis for the modern HAZUS General Building Stock, which saw a ~44% jump in per-capita building exposure after the NSI update).  NFIP policy/penetration data for flood.
- **Population & social vulnerability:** **CDC/ATSDR Social Vulnerability Index** and the BRIC community-resilience index — both used inside the NRI. The IHP-equity literature (ScienceDirect S2212420922000747) shows race/ethnicity and socioeconomic indicators predict IA receipt even *controlling for damage*, so these are legitimate predictive features (with the ethical caveat that they encode existing inequities).
- **Bureaucratic/behavioral features (the non-obvious, high-value ones from the PA outlays paper):** state, county’s count of prior declarations, declaration timing/lag, per-capita impact relative to the PA threshold.
- **Cost-share/program structure:** the standard 75/25 federal/non-federal share governs PA federal-share obligations; FEMA’s own statewide per capita indicator for *recommending* raising the federal cost share from 75% to as high as 90% (under the Stafford Act) is **$189 for major disasters declared on or after January 1, 2026** (Federal Register notice 2026-08224, FEMA, published 04/28/2026: “the statewide per capita indicator for recommending cost share adjustments for major disasters declared on or after January 1, 2026… is $189”). This cost-share toggle is itself a predictable feature your model can encode.

#### A4. Validation

- **Backtest by temporal hold-out** (train on older disasters, test on recent) and by **leave-one-disaster-out** cross-validation — not random row splits, which leak within-event correlation.
- **Metrics for skewed data:** report error on both log and natural scales; use MAE/RMSE on log dollars, plus distribution-aware metrics. Borrow the **Gini index / Lorenz curve** approach standard in actuarial ratemaking, and calibration plots (predicted vs. actual obligation deciles). Report the occurrence stage with AUROC and the magnitude stage with R²/RMSE, mirroring the tornado paper’s reporting so reviewers can benchmark you.
- **Honest uncertainty:** produce prediction *intervals*, not point estimates; the long tail means point accuracy will be modest (recall the tornado model’s conditional R²≈0.43). 
- **Sanity-anchor to the macro total:** PA awarded >$125B (2000–2019, 2020 dollars) per the *Natural Hazards* paper; federal disaster spending rose from roughly $19B (2016) to $33B (2025) in inflation-adjusted terms per Cato — your county-level predictions should aggregate sensibly to such totals.

#### A5. Prior art to differentiate against

- **HAZUS** (FEMA, free, ArcGIS Pro–based, now v7.x): engineering fragility/damage functions for earthquake, hurricane wind, flood, tsunami;  estimates *physical and economic* loss, not FEMA obligations. Its own manuals concede large uncertainty from incomplete inventories. Differentiator: HAZUS tells you expected building damage; it does not tell you eligible federal cost.
- **National Risk Index** (FEMA, free on OpenFEMA, v1.20 Dec 2025): Expected Annual Loss = Exposure × Annualized Frequency × Historic Loss Ratio, modulated by SVI and resilience. It is an *annualized, prospective* risk score, not an *event-specific obligation* estimate.
- **Commercial cat models — Verisk (Touchstone→Synergy Studio 2026), Moody’s RMS, CoreLogic, KatRisk:** probabilistic stochastic event sets → hazard → vulnerability → financial module, built to price *insurance* and reinsurance. Moody’s Corp. paid approximately **$2.0 billion (£1.425 billion) to acquire RMS from Britain’s Daily Mail and General Trust** — announced Aug 5 and closed Sept 15, 2021 (Insurance Journal). A flood-model validation study (Journal of Catastrophe Risk and Resilience) found Verisk, KatRisk, and Moody’s RMS within ~4% of NFIP historical losses — these are formidable on *insured* loss but say nothing about FEMA grant obligations and are expensive, opaque, and insurance-oriented.
- **Climate-analytics startups — First Street, Jupiter Intelligence, XDI:** property-level forward-looking physical climate risk, sold to real estate/finance. First Street closed an oversubscribed **$46M Series A/A-1 on July 25, 2024, led by Innovation Endeavors (with SE Ventures and Nuveen Real Estate)**, per Axios (“It has since taken in $46 million in total funding in the Series A from partners including Galvanize Climate Solutions and Congruent Ventures”). They translate hazard to *damage/financial loss*, not federal obligations, and a Bloomberg/CarbonPlan investigation showed their models disagree materially and are largely proprietary black boxes — strengthening the case for a *transparent* niche tool.
- **Parametric insurance / ART — Descartes, Arbol, FloodFlash, Swiss Re, Munich Re:** trigger-based payouts on wind speed/flood depth/rainfall. Arbol **paid $20M in parametric reinsurance claims, all within 30 days of Hurricane Milton’s Oct 9, 2024 landfall near Siesta Key (Cat 3, 120 mph sustained winds)**, per Artemis.bm (“Arbol… paid $20 million to clients that saw their parametric reinsurance arrangements triggered by hurricane Milton, with these payouts all made within 30 days of the storm’s landfall”). They care about *their own* trigger calibration and basis risk, not FEMA payouts per se — but the FEMA Review Council’s RAPID parametric-PA proposal makes FEMA-obligation calibration directly relevant to them.

**The white space:** a transparent, event-conditioned estimator of *FEMA PA/IA obligated dollars*, specialized to Region 5 small events, delivering a fast “expedited funding estimate” with comparables and intervals. No incumbent does exactly this.

### PART B — MARKET & POSITIONING

#### B6. Who would buy/value this

**Tier 1 — Disaster-recovery consulting firms (the bullseye).** Firms like Hagerty Consulting, Tidal Basin (now part of a group with Michael Baker International and MLU Services),  IEM, Tetra Tech, ICF, CDR/Lemoine, AECOM, Guidehouse, and Witt O’Brien’s make money managing FEMA PA/IA grants for state/local/tribal/PNP clients — developing damage inventories, scopes of work, cost estimates,  and maximizing/expediting obligations, often funded through FEMA management costs or Direct Administrative Costs. Hagerty alone reports supporting ~150 SLTT/PNP clients in obtaining/retaining over $69B in PA funding across 27 states since 2001. An obligation-prediction tool helps them (a) **scope and price** engagements (fee often scales with recovery size/complexity), (b) **win bids** by quantifying likely recovery to a prospective client immediately after an event, (c) **set client cash-flow expectations**, and (d) **triage** which small Region 5 events justify mobilization. This is the audience whose day-to-day economics the tool most directly improves.

**Tier 2 — Parametric/reinsurance/ART players (Descartes, Arbol, Raincoat, Swiss Re, Munich Re, cat-bond sponsors).** Historically they care about their own triggers, not FEMA payouts. But the May 2026 FEMA Review Council parametric-PA (“RAPID”) proposal would make government disaster payouts themselves parametric — so a model of historical FEMA obligations vs. event parameters is exactly the calibration/basis-risk evidence base needed to design those triggers and avoid over/underpayment (a risk the CRS and FAS commentaries flag explicitly, citing Harvey/Ike where wind category understated damage).

**Tier 3 — Cat-risk and climate-analytics firms (Verisk, Moody’s RMS, CoreLogic, First Street, Jupiter).** Less likely to *buy the product*, more likely to value the *skill set and the dataset-engineering* — i.e., acquisition of talent. These firms hire people who can fuse NOAA/USGS hazard data with exposure inventories and loss outcomes; a public portfolio piece is a direct hiring signal.

**Tier 4 — State/local emergency management and govtech vendors.** State emergency management agencies, county EM offices, and grant-management software vendors (e.g., the Odysseus GMT, Tetra Tech’s RecoveryTrac) could embed obligation estimates for budgeting and declaration cost-benefit decisions — especially salient as raised declaration thresholds push more borderline Region 5 events below the federal line, forcing states to self-assess. For context on where that line sits today: FEMA’s **statewide per capita impact indicator is $1.89 and the countywide indicator $4.72** (with a small-project minimum of $4,000 and a large-project threshold of $1,062,900), per FEMA guidance distributed via the Vermont League of Cities and Towns (“The State per capita indicator will be $1.89. The County per capita indicator will be $4.72”). The FRC proposes raising these indicators, which would push more small Midwest events below the federal threshold.

**Tier 5 — Public finance / muni risk / resilience investors.** Municipal CFOs, bond analysts, and resilience investors increasingly price physical risk; a tool estimating federal backstop dollars after an event informs contingent-liability and recovery-financing analysis. This is a smaller, longer-sales-cycle audience.

#### B7. How comparable software is positioned and sold

- **Cat models / climate analytics:** enterprise SaaS + data licensing + expert services, high ACV, sold to insurers/reinsurers/financial institutions; methodology often proprietary (First Street is a partial exception, publishing methodology while paywalling high-resolution data). Moody’s RMS runs the cloud “Intelligent Risk Platform”; Verisk licenses Touchstone/Synergy Studio.
- **Govtech SaaS:** long procurement cycles (commonly 12–18 months),  budget-season-driven, and gated by **cooperative purchasing vehicles** (NASPO ValuePoint, OMNIA Partners, GSA cooperative purchasing/Schedules, TIPS, state DIR contracts).  Cold RFP responses are low-ROI for early-stage vendors; relationships + getting onto a cooperative vehicle is the scalable path.  Government software spend is large and growing. 
- **Implication for the user:** the realistic monetization is *not* a self-serve product sold to governments while employed; it is (a) a credibility asset that lands a role/contract at a Tier 1–3 firm, or (b) eventually a services-plus-tool consulting offering after leaving FEMA.

#### B8. Portfolio strategy for a government analyst

What makes such a project legible and impressive to hiring managers and firm principals:

- **A public, interactive demo** (map/dashboard) where a user inputs event conditions for a real or hypothetical Region 5 event and sees estimated PA/IA obligations with intervals and the top-5 analog disasters. The tornado-damage paper’s interactive gridded maps are a model; an open Streamlit/Observable/ArcGIS dashboard signals product sense.
- **A transparent methodology write-up** (blog + GitHub repo) with the data pipeline, model, backtest results, and *honest* error metrics. Transparency is itself the differentiator against black-box cat models and is what the academic and CarbonPlan critiques reward.
- **Domain credibility framing:** “I work in FEMA PA recovery; here is a data-driven view of what drives obligations” — but conveyed *without* leaning on the official title in a way that implies FEMA endorsement (see ethics below).
- **Reproducibility:** all public data, documented joins, runnable code — exactly what FEMA’s own openfema-samples repo models and what cat-risk/govtech engineering managers screen for.

**Ethics and conflict-of-interest navigation (general guidance, not legal advice — and the employee must get a written determination from the FEMA servicing ethics office / Chief Deputy Ethics Official before launching):**

- **Prior written approval is the default.** The DHS Supplemental Standards, **5 CFR 4601.103(a)**, require a DHS employee to “obtain prior written approval before engaging in any outside employment or activity … with or without compensation,” unless the activity falls in an exempted category. DHS has a memo exempting certain low-conflict categories, but a FEMA-program-centric data project may be scrutinized because the regulation excludes from “outside activity” anything “relating to any ongoing or announced policy, program, or operation of the employee’s agency” (5 CFR 4601.101).
- **FEMA-specific contractor bar:** **5 CFR 4601.105(b)** — “no non-intermittent FEMA employee shall be employed by a current FEMA contractor” (waivable only in writing by the FEMA Chief Deputy Ethics Official, with DAEO concurrence). This directly constrains moonlighting for, or being hired by, the very Tier 1 consulting firms that are FEMA PA contractors *while still employed by FEMA*.
- **OGE-450 disclosure:** A Recovery appeals analyst whose duties affect non-Federal entities’ financial interests is commonly designated a confidential financial-disclosure (OGE Form 450) filer under 5 CFR 2634, Subpart I; outside positions, business entities, and client income would be reportable, and the filing is the checkpoint where ethics officials catch venture conflicts.
- **Misuse-of-position rules (5 CFR Part 2635, Subpart G):** no use of **nonpublic information** — 2635.703(a): “Employees may not engage in financial transactions using nonpublic information, nor allow the improper use of nonpublic information to further their own private interests or those of another, whether through advice or recommendation, or by knowing unauthorized disclosure”; no use of **public office for private gain** or appearance of **governmental sanction** (2635.702 — don’t use the FEMA title/seal to market it); no use of **government property** (2635.704) or **official time** (2635.705) — build it entirely on personal equipment, on personal time, with only public OpenFEMA/NOAA data.
- **The big legal line — 18 U.S.C. 205/203:** while employed, the user may **not** act as agent or be compensated to represent any private party before FEMA (or any federal agency) on appeals/claims/applications  — even unpaid representation is barred by §205, with penalties under 18 U.S.C. 216. A *data/analytics* product is fine; *using it to advocate for specific clients before FEMA* is prohibited until after leaving and clearing post-employment cooling-off restrictions.
- **Intellectual property is favorable:** under **17 U.S.C. 105/101**, a work created on the employee’s “own volition and outside his or her duties” (House Report 94-1476) is *not* a U.S. Government work even if the subject matter relates to the government field, so the employee can hold copyright in personally-built code/analysis — reinforcing the need to keep it strictly off official time/equipment.
- **OpenFEMA Terms & Conditions:** public-domain data may be used commercially with attribution, but require the disclaimer “This product uses the Federal Emergency Management Agency’s OpenFEMA API, but is not endorsed by FEMA,” bar using data to make individual eligibility/rights determinations, and bar re-identification. The required non-endorsement disclaimer conveniently also satisfies the 2635.702(b) “appearance of sanction” concern.

#### B9. Honest value proposition and limitations

**What it can credibly claim:** a fast, transparent, comparables-driven estimate of *likely FEMA PA/IA obligated dollars* for a current or hypothetical event, specialized to Region 5 small-to-mid events, with explicit uncertainty intervals and named analog disasters — answering a question HAZUS, NRI, and cat models do not address, and directly useful for consulting scoping, state declaration cost-benefit, and parametric-trigger calibration.

**Limitations to disclose up front:**

- Obligation data lags and excludes open projects, so recent-event training labels are incomplete and the model under-predicts immature disasters.
- Storm Events damage inputs are noisy “best guesses” with heavy missingness; the model inherits that noise.
- The magnitude stage explains under half the variance (cf. tornado R²≈0.43); point estimates carry wide intervals, especially for rare severe events.
- Selection bias: the model is trained only on *declared* events, where declaration is itself partly political/bureaucratic.
- Predictions reflect *historical* obligation behavior; the FEMA Review Council reforms (RAPID/FAIR, higher thresholds) could structurally change how dollars flow, so the model needs a clearly labeled “pre-reform regime” caveat and periodic re-training.
- SVI-based features encode existing inequities in aid distribution; using them improves prediction but must be framed carefully.

## Recommendations

**Stage 1 — Clear the runway (before writing code).** Submit a written outside-activity request to the FEMA servicing ethics office describing the project, confirm your OGE-450 status, and get a written determination. Build only on personal equipment/time with public data. **Threshold that changes the plan:** if ethics declines approval or your duties create an unavoidable conflict, pivot to a *de-identified, methodology-only* publication with no live tool until you depart FEMA.

**Stage 2 — Build the minimum credible model (8–12 weeks of evenings/weekends).** (1) Assemble the OpenFEMA join (Disaster Declarations Summaries v2 ⨝ PA Funded Projects Details v2 ⨝ PA Applicants ⨝ IHP) for FEMA Regions covering IL/IN/MI/MN/OH/WI; (2) attach NOAA Storm Events + USGS streamgage + USA Structures/NSI + CDC SVI features at county-disaster grain; (3) fit a Tweedie-loss LightGBM/CatBoost model plus a logistic+gamma hurdle baseline; (4) backtest with leave-one-disaster-out and temporal hold-out; (5) report log- and natural-scale error honestly. **Benchmark to beat:** occurrence AUROC > 0.82 and conditional magnitude R² in the 0.4–0.5 range would match/exceed the published tornado model and is a credible “ship it” bar.

**Stage 3 — Package for legibility.** Ship a public GitHub repo + write-up + an interactive map/dashboard with the analog-events feature and uncertainty intervals, carrying the required OpenFEMA non-endorsement disclaimer and a personal-capacity disclaimer. Write one plain-language post (“What actually drives FEMA obligations in the Midwest”) aimed at consulting principals.

**Stage 4 — Convert to opportunity.** Target Tier 1 consulting firms (Hagerty, Tidal Basin, IEM, Tetra Tech, ICF) and Tier 2/3 parametric/cat-risk firms for roles or post-employment contracts — *not* sales while employed. **Threshold that escalates to a real product:** if (a) the FEMA Review Council’s RAPID parametric-PA model advances toward implementation, or (b) a Tier-1 firm expresses procurement interest, formalize via a cooperative-purchasing path (NASPO/OMNIA/GSA) and a services-plus-tool model *after* leaving federal service and clearing post-employment restrictions.

**Stage 5 — Maintain credibility.** Re-train as obligations mature and as FEMA reform changes the regime; version the model and label the policy regime it was trained on.

## Caveats

- The single most important external uncertainty is **FEMA reform**: if PA becomes a parametric block grant and IA a single direct payment (FRC report, May 7, 2026), the *historical* obligation relationship the tool learns may partly break — which is simultaneously a risk (model drift) and an opportunity (trigger-calibration demand). Implementation requires acts of Congress  and is uncertain; H.R. 4669 (FEMA Act of 2025) is a parallel track.
- Public reporting does not give a precise dollar figure for the disaster-recovery *consulting* market size or per-engagement fee economics; the firm-revenue claims here are anchored to firms’ own published figures (e.g., Hagerty’s $69B retained-funding figure) and should be treated as illustrative, not audited.
- Ethics guidance here is general and synthesized from primary sources (5 CFR 4601, 5 CFR 2635, 5 CFR 2634, 18 U.S.C. 203/205, 17 U.S.C. 105); the applicability of the prior-approval exemption categories and OGE-450 designation is position- and fact-specific and must be confirmed with FEMA’s ethics office.
- Model accuracy claims from the literature (tornado AUROC 0.872, R² 0.432) are for *property damage*, not *FEMA obligations* specifically; obligation prediction may be harder (more bureaucratic noise) or easier (coarser county grain) — this is an empirical question the user’s backtest will answer.
- Threshold figures vary slightly by source and fiscal year: the FY2026 statewide per capita indicator is reported as $1.89 (with countywide $4.72) for declaration recommendations, while a separate, higher per capita indicator ($189) governs the 75%→90% cost-share adjustment recommendation; do not conflate the two.
